Prologue

The Last Open Port

By the summer of 1864, the Confederacy was bleeding from a thousand wounds. Vicksburg had fallen. Atlanta was besieged. The Army of Northern Virginia was pinned in the trenches of Petersburg. But on the Gulf Coast of Alabama, one great prize still flew Confederate colors: Mobile, the South's most important remaining port on the Gulf of Mexico.

From Havana, Cuba, blockade runners carried vital supplies — salt, medicine, arms — into Mobile Bay. Two railroads connected the city to the Confederacy's interior, feeding the war machine that was running out of everything. Ulysses S. Grant, commanding all Union armies, had made its capture a priority. The man he entrusted with the naval assault was Rear Admiral David Glasgow Farragut, a 63-year-old sailor who had entered the Navy at age nine and spent fifty-four years afloat.

The Confederacy had transformed the approaches to Mobile Bay into a nearly impregnable gauntlet. Fort Morgan on Mobile Point bristled with 46 heavy guns. Fort Gaines stood on Dauphin Island. Between them, the Confederate Torpedo Bureau had planted 67 naval mines — "torpedoes," as they were called — in a triple line across the channel, leaving only a narrow passage of open water directly under Fort Morgan's guns. And waiting inside the bay was the CSS Tennessee, said to be the most powerful ironclad afloat, commanded by the Confederacy's only full admiral: the redoubtable Franklin Buchanan.

What followed — the Battle of Mobile Bay in August 1864, the siege of Spanish Fort and the fall of Fort Blakeley in spring 1865 — was a campaign that would close one of history's great naval harbors, settle the fate of a city that had defied the Union for three years, and end, on a bluff overlooking the Mobile-Tensaw Delta, with the very last major battle of the American Civil War.

Union

Rear Admiral David G. Farragut
Commander, West Gulf Blockading Squadron

Born in Tennessee but fiercely loyal to the Union, Farragut had entered the Navy at age nine. He had captured New Orleans in 1862 and spent the intervening years pressing for the attack on Mobile Bay. Now 63, he would lead from the rigging of his flagship USS Hartford — lashed to the mast so he could see above the smoke — through the most daring naval action of the war.

Confederate

Admiral Franklin Buchanan
Commander, Confederate Naval Forces, Mobile

The Confederacy's only full admiral, Buchanan had commanded the CSS Virginia at Hampton Roads in 1862, the first day of the famous ironclad duel, and been wounded there. Now flying his flag from the CSS Tennessee — the most powerful ironclad the South ever built — he waited inside Mobile Bay to defend it at any cost. He would be wounded again, and captured, before the day was out.

Mobile Bay — August 5, 1864

The naval battle at the mouth of the bay and the subsequent reduction of the forts

August 5, 1864 dawned with a gentle haze that turned the sky a milky white and a sea as smooth as glass. Just before 5:30 in the morning, Rear Admiral Farragut's fleet of eighteen warships got under way, standing into the mouth of Mobile Bay in two parallel columns. The four ironclad monitors steamed on the right, nearest Fort Morgan and its 46 guns. The fourteen wooden warships, lashed in pairs so one could tow the other if disabled, followed on the left — each bigger ship shielding its smaller partner.

Farragut himself had planned to lead the assault from his flagship USS Hartford but at the last minute, his captains prevailed upon him to let the sloop Brooklyn go first. If the admiral were hurt early, they argued, the whole attack might collapse. Reluctantly, he agreed. He climbed into the rigging of Hartford to see above the smoke and haze, and was lashed there for his safety.

At 6:45 a.m., the lead ironclad, Tecumseh, steamed within extreme range of Fort Morgan's guns. The fort's defenders began to reply. By 7:15, the action was general — the roar of the guns, the shriek of shot, the white smoke beginning to obscure everything in a choking fog of battle.

The Sinking of the Tecumseh

Captain Tunis Craven in the Tecumseh spotted the CSS Tennessee through the narrow viewing slit of his pilothouse. Intent on engaging Buchanan's ironclad, he steered Tecumseh slightly to the right — directly into the minefield. At approximately 7:40 a.m., the monitor struck a torpedo. The explosion was enormous. Tecumseh rolled over and sank in less than a minute, carrying 94 of her 114 men — including Captain Craven — to the bottom of Mobile Bay. In a last selfless act, Craven stood aside to let his pilot through the hatch first. There was not time for both.

"As the monitor sank, only 21 men reached the surface. Farragut, watching from Hartford's rigging, immediately dispatched a small boat to rescue survivors even as Fort Morgan's guns trained on it." — American Battlefield Trust

The sudden destruction of Tecumseh caused confusion in the Union line. Captain Alden of Brooklyn, seeing the mine explode, panicked and reversed engines to avoid the wreck and any other torpedoes. Behind Brooklyn, the remaining ships slowed and bunched up — directly under the guns of Fort Morgan. Confederate gunners lashed at the massed Federal squadron and were finding the range. Disaster loomed minutes away.

"Damn the Torpedoes"

Farragut watched all of this from Hartford's rigging. He could not stop under the guns of Fort Morgan. He could not back down with a column of ships behind him. He ordered Hartford to pull out of line and steam past the stalled Brooklyn to port — directly through the minefield.

"Damn the torpedoes! Four bells, Captain Drayton!" — and to the commander of the Metacomet, lashed to Hartford's side — "Jouett, full speed!" — Most reliable reconstruction of Farragut's famous order, August 5, 1864

Hartford and Metacomet put on maximum power, their propellers lashing the water together. As they passed through the minefield, some Union sailors later claimed they had heard the primers snapping on the torpedoes — clicking against Hartford's hull as she steamed through without exploding them. Several had been submerged too long in the warm water to function. The gamble paid off. The entire column of warships followed in Hartford's wake, and every ship made it through.

Once inside the expansive bay, the fleet could quickly move out of range of Fort Morgan's guns, leaving only the Confederate ships to contend with. Farragut's gunboats promptly captured the CSS Selma and drove CSS Gaines onto the beach, where her crew burned her. CSS Morgan fled to the protection of the fort's guns. Only the Tennessee remained.

The Lone Charge of the CSS Tennessee

Farragut expected Admiral Buchanan to shelter Tennessee under Fort Morgan's guns while the Union fleet anchored in the upper bay and assessed its damage. What happened next astonished every officer on both sides. Instead of retreating to safety, Buchanan made his decision: he would take on the entire Federal fleet, single-handed.

The CSS Tennessee steamed directly for the Union fleet. She was slow — barely six knots — but her armor was six inches of iron over twenty-four inches of timber, and her two seven-inch and four 6.4-inch Brooke rifles had already demonstrated their lethal effectiveness. Buchanan hoped to ram his adversaries as he had at Hampton Roads; this time, his target ships were in motion and his own vessel was the slower. Tennessee became the target of ramming rather than the rammer.

For the next hour, the entire Union fleet — monitors, sloops, gunboats — concentrated on the lone Confederate ironclad. The Monongahela rammed her. The Lackawanna rammed her twice. Shot after shot bounced off Tennessee's iron sides — until Union gunners found the ship's fatal weakness: her exposed rudder chains, running in unprotected trenches across the after deck. Shot carried them away, leaving the ironclad unable to steer. She could only circle helplessly as the Union fleet pounded her into submission.

"Admiral Buchanan suffered a badly broken leg from flying iron fragments. No longer able to command, he gave permission to surrender. A little more than three hours had elapsed since Tecumseh fired the first shot." — Encyclopedia of Alabama

The Confederate flag came down from the Tennessee. The Battle of Mobile Bay — the naval engagement — was over. Mobile Bay now belonged to the Union. Fort Gaines surrendered on August 8. Fort Morgan, after a two-week bombardment, surrendered on August 23. The last open Confederate port on the Gulf of Mexico was closed. Mobile would not fall for another eight months — but without its harbor, it was already dying.

"Farragut's victory at Mobile Bay was one of the badly needed Union victories that, alongside Atlanta and Cedar Creek, ensured President Abraham Lincoln's reelection in November 1864 and turned the tide of the war in the Union's favor."
— American Battlefield Trust

Union

Maj. Gen. Edward R. S. Canby
Commander, Army of West Mississippi

Canby commanded the most powerful force ever assembled on the Gulf Coast — 32,000 men of the XIII and XVI Corps. Moving north from Fish River along the eastern shore of Mobile Bay, he aimed to reduce the Confederate defenses one by one and take the city that had defied the Union for three years. His methodical approach, criticized by Grant as too slow, was about to be tested against an unexpectedly fierce defense.

Confederate

Brig. Gen. Randall L. Gibson
Commander, Spanish Fort Garrison

Only 33 years old, Gibson commanded six Louisiana infantry regiments and five Alabama infantry regiments in a fortified position outnumbered ten to one. He "considerably astonished" his Union opponents with the ferocity of his defense, holding Spanish Fort for twelve days before executing a brilliantly planned nighttime evacuation that saved most of his command from capture.

Spanish Fort — The Siege Lines

The progressive Union encirclement of the Confederate defenses, March 27 – April 8, 1865

The land campaign to take Mobile began on March 13, 1865, when a massive column of 32,000 Union soldiers started moving north from Fish River on the eastern side of Mobile Bay. Some came by water; others marched up from Fort Morgan. A second column of 13,000 men under Brig. Gen. Frederick Steele departed Pensacola, swinging west toward the Tensaw River and then south to invest Fort Blakeley from the rear, giving the impression it was headed toward Montgomery before turning toward the bay.

Spanish Fort sat on a commanding hilltop with a broad flat summit, steep sides, and a 360-degree view of the surrounding terrain. The Confederates had constructed trenches and artillery redoubts in a semicircle two miles long and 1,000 yards wide between Minette Bay and D'Olive Bay. Fort McDermott guarded the land approaches. Old Spanish Fort — built on the site of the original Spanish fortification of 1780, nearly a century earlier — covered the river approaches. Six Louisiana infantry regiments under Gibson and five Alabama infantry regiments together held these works. Their total strength was perhaps 2,500 men against Canby's 32,000.

The Siege Tightens

When Canby's forces rendezvoused at Danley's Ferry on March 27 and immediately began to invest Spanish Fort, General Gibson was outnumbered approximately ten to one. The Union forces had enveloped the fort by April 1 and began constructing their classic siege works: three parallel systems of earthworks, each one closer to the Confederate position than the last. Engineers worked night and day. The armies skirmished constantly. By April 8, Canby had ninety cannon aimed at the Confederate lines, compared to the forty-seven available to Gibson's defenders — many of which were positioned to defend against a water-borne attack and could not be turned to face the land threat.

"Despite being outnumbered about 10 to 1, 33-year-old Brig. Gen. Randall Lee Gibson mounted a skillful and spirited defense that 'considerably astonished' his Union opponents." — Paul Brueske, Digging All Night and Fighting All Day (2024)

On April 8, the Federals opened a massive bombardment. Confederate guns replied, and throughout the day flame and smoke enveloped the battlefield as the ground shook for miles around. Late that afternoon, the 8th Iowa Infantry broke through the Confederate lines at the northern end of the battlefield. Gibson knew the position could not be held another day.

The Night Evacuation

Under cover of darkness on the night of April 8–9, Gibson executed a brilliantly managed withdrawal. The Confederates spiked their guns, destroyed the gun carriages, and slipped away toward the Blakeley River. Many made their way to Mobile by boat. Gibson sent approximately 1,000 troops to reinforce the garrison at Fort Blakeley, five miles to the north. About midnight, federal forces realized the vast majority of the Confederates had left. Canby's men captured approximately 500 men who had not escaped and large quantities of artillery shells and gunpowder.

The fall of Spanish Fort, which had stood against overwhelming Union forces for twelve days, freed Canby to concentrate his entire strength on one last obstacle: Fort Blakeley, where the final act of the Mobile Campaign — and of the Civil War itself — was already beginning.

"With a force of only a couple of thousand men, they defended Spanish Fort for 12 days against a Union army 15 times the size of their own."
— Explore Southern History

Steele's column from Pensacola arrived at Fort Blakeley on April 1, having marched through muddy spring roads after fighting several sharp engagements. His 1st Division under Brig. Gen. John P. Hawkins included three brigades of United States Colored Troops — former enslaved and free Black men who would play a decisive role in the assault. With Spanish Fort fallen, Canby now concentrated 16,000 men against the final Confederate stronghold.

Confederate

Brig. Gen. St. John R. Liddell
Commander, Fort Blakeley Garrison

Liddell commanded roughly 3,500 men in a nine-redoubt defensive line stretching nearly three miles across the pine-covered bluffs overlooking the Mobile-Tensaw Delta. His force included veteran Missouri and Mississippi troops under Brig. Gen. Francis M. Cockrell and Alabama Brigade reserves — many of them teenage conscripts fighting their first and last battle on the last official day of the Civil War.

Fort Blakeley — The Final Assault

The nine Confederate redoubts, three Union parallels, and the 5:30 p.m. charge of April 9, 1865

Fort Blakeley was, in 1865, a ghost town wearing a uniform. The city of Blakeley had been chartered in 1814 and for a decade had rivaled Mobile as a Gulf Coast port of consequence. Then yellow fever arrived. The population collapsed. The buildings rotted. By the time Confederate engineers surveyed the site in 1862, looking for positions to defend Mobile's eastern approaches, they found an impressive hilltop overlooking the Mobile-Tensaw Delta and almost nothing else. What they built there would become the site of the last major battle of the American Civil War.

The fort's nine connected redoubts and linking breastworks stretched nearly three miles across the pine-covered bluffs, mounting forty-one artillery pieces including mortars and protected by Confederate ironclads on the adjacent river. General Liddell's 3,500 defenders — veterans from Missouri and Mississippi mixed with Alabama reserves, many of them teenagers — had been watching the siege of Spanish Fort to the south with growing unease. When Spanish Fort fell on the night of April 8, and Gibson's survivors began arriving at Blakeley, everyone understood what was coming.

The United States Colored Troops

The Union force assembling against Fort Blakeley included approximately 5,000 men of the United States Colored Troops — one of the largest concentrations of African American soldiers in any single battle of the Civil War. They had marched from Pensacola with Steele's column, fighting sharp engagements along the way through muddy spring roads. Many had been enslaved men. Some had been free Black men from New Orleans, whose ancestors had been in Louisiana for generations. Their nine regiments — the 47th, 48th, 50th, 51st, 68th, 73rd, 76th, 82nd, and 86th Infantries — were organized into one division under Brig. Gen. John P. Hawkins and positioned on the right flank of the Union army, fronting Redoubts 1 and 2 on the Confederate left.

The 73rd Regiment of the USCT — composed of men largely from New Orleans, among the first Black troops accepted into the Union army — traced its origins to a free Black militia unit that had actually offered itself for Confederate service at the beginning of the war and been refused on account of race. Now, at Fort Blakeley, its officers Lt. Col. Henry C. Merriam and Capt. Henry C. Nichols would volunteer to lead their men in a reconnaissance of the Confederate lines in advance of the assault — and both would be awarded the Medal of Honor.

"Hawkins' men probed the Confederate left on the afternoon of April 9, 1865, becoming involved in heavy skirmishing hours before the general assault on the rebel lines at 5:30 PM." — Historic Blakeley State Park historical marker, USCT at Fort Blakeley

5:30 PM — The Last Charge

Canby passed the order along the lines: a sweeping, united charge at 5:30 PM on April 9. He was afraid the Confederates might vanish overnight as they had at Spanish Fort — and he would not let them. Unknown to every soldier on both sides, that same afternoon, 800 miles to the northeast at a farmhouse called Appomattox Court House in Virginia, Robert E. Lee was signing the surrender of the Army of Northern Virginia to Ulysses S. Grant.

Across a nearly three-mile front, federal troops emerged from their trenches in places less than 1,000 yards from the Confederate works and charged. They began taking casualties almost immediately — rifle fire, artillery, and the land mines planted in the ground before the Confederate works. Men tripped the mines and died yards from where they started. But the surging mass kept moving.

The breakthrough came when a Union brigade led by the 83rd Ohio Infantry stormed over the walls of Redoubt 4. Capt. Patrick H. Pentzer of the 97th Illinois was the first man into Redoubt 4, and received the personal surrender of Brig. Gen. Francis M. Cockrell inside — earning his Medal of Honor in the last few minutes of the last major battle of the war. The Confederate lines collapsed. Union troops poured in. Men who didn't surrender fled through the thicket beyond their works or ran toward the river. Some reached Mobile. Others were captured. Some drowned trying to escape.

"Probably the last charge of this war, it was as gallant as any on record... The loss here was great; but no obstacle hindered, no degree of carnage daunted the men." — Harper's Weekly, on the assault at Fort Blakeley, 1865

When the smoke cleared, approximately 75 Confederate defenders lay dead. Over 2,800 were prisoners of war. Union casualties during the full siege and assault were approximately 150 killed and 650 wounded. Thirteen soldiers received the Congressional Medal of Honor for their actions at Fort Blakeley. Liddell surrendered his command to Canby approximately six hours after Lee had surrendered at Appomattox — six hours after, by most accounts, the Civil War had already ended.

The Aftermath

Some Confederate soldiers in Redoubts 1 and 2, where the USCT had charged, were afraid they would not be treated fairly and rushed to neighboring redoubts to surrender to white troops instead. In the chaos of the final minutes, some Union USCT soldiers did fire on Confederates who had already surrendered — the rage of men whose comrades had been massacred by Confederate troops at Fort Pillow, Tennessee, less than a year earlier, surfacing in the final moments of the final battle. Union officers moved to stop it; one was killed and another wounded in the effort. Order was quickly restored. The incident left a shadow, but no sanction.

Two days after Fort Blakeley fell, Batteries Huger and Tracy were abandoned. On April 12, 1865, Mobile Mayor Robert H. Slough surrendered the city without a fight. Gen. Dabney Maury had evacuated his remaining 4,500 men on the evening of April 11. The last great Confederate port on the Gulf of Mexico — the city that had defied the Union for three years, that Farragut had sealed off the previous August, that 32,000 soldiers had marched through Baldwin County to take — was at last in Union hands.

Legacy

After the Guns

The Campaign That Closed an Era

Why These Battles Matter

The Last Confederate Port

Mobile Bay's closure in August 1864 eliminated the Confederacy's last significant Gulf port, cutting off vital supplies from Havana and the Caribbean. The psychological and strategic blow to the South was immense — and arriving alongside Atlanta's fall, it sealed Lincoln's reelection.

The Last Major Naval Battle

The Battle of Mobile Bay was the last major naval engagement of the Civil War. Farragut's "Damn the Torpedoes" has entered American culture as a lasting expression of audacity under fire — though scholars still debate what he actually said, and whether anyone could have heard him above the guns.

The Last Major Land Battle

Fort Blakeley on April 9, 1865, was the last combined-force battle of the Civil War — fought, unknown to either army, hours after Lee's surrender at Appomattox. The men who died there died for a cause that had already ended. They did not know it yet.

The USCT at Blakeley

The role of 5,000 United States Colored Troops in the assault on Fort Blakeley represents one of the most significant concentrations of African American soldiers in any battle of the war. Men who had been enslaved charged the earthworks of their former world and helped end it.

Baldwin County's Place in History

The same ground where the Baldwin County Historical Society would one day preserve the family stories of the Wynns, the Pierces, and the Sharretts was the theater of the Civil War's final act. Spanish Fort, where condominiums now cover the earthworks, and Blakeley, still preserved in state parkland, are where the long war ended.

Historic Blakeley State Park

Today 3,800 acres of the original battlefield are preserved at Historic Blakeley State Park north of Spanish Fort — the largest National Register of Historic Places site east of the Mississippi River. Visitors can still walk the Confederate earthworks, the Union parallels, and the terrain of Redoubt 4, where the last charge of the Civil War crested a hill overlooking the Mobile-Tensaw Delta.

The Men Who Were There

In the records of the Baldwin County Historical Society, there are names that echo through these same years. The Pumphrey boys who served in later wars. The fishermen of Bon Secour who would have known about the storm that swept Navy Cove in 1906. The Sonora settlers who arrived just decades after these battles. The community that built itself in the longleaf pines south and east of Blakeley had already lived through the war that left those earthworks behind.

At Historic Blakeley State Park today, visitors can walk the Confederate earthworks — still visible after 160 years, still rising out of the Alabama clay in the pine-scented air above the Mobile-Tensaw Delta. They can trace the Union parallels. They can stand at Redoubt 4 and look across the same ground that 16,000 men charged on an April afternoon while the war was already over, while the news was making its way south, while men were still dying.

The site is the largest National Register of Historic Places site east of the Mississippi River. E. O. Wilson, the biologist and Alabama native, wrote a letter to The Conservation Fund about the importance of this land — not only for history but for its ecology, the hardwood cove ravines and blackwater swamps and pine uplands that had survived because they were a battlefield, because no one wanted to build on them. The dead had preserved the living.

"The Battle of Fort Blakeley concluded a few hours after Lee signed over the Army of Northern Virginia. Some estimate that U.S. Colored Troops represented nearly half the Union force at Fort Blakeley. The involvement of these troops in critical battles such as this has gone largely untold." — The Conservation Fund

The men of the 73rd USCT — descendants of a free Black militia that had been refused service at the beginning of a war they were finally allowed to fight at its very end — charged those earthworks knowing what they were charging toward and what they were charging away from. Six hours after Appomattox. On the last day the Civil War was fought. On the ground of the old county seat of Baldwin County, where yellow fever had already done what the Union army was finishing.

The last charge of the last battle of the last campaign ended here, on the bluffs above the Tensaw River, in the spring of 1865, where the azaleas were already blooming.

"On April 9, 1865 — the last official day of the Civil War — in less than half an hour, Fort Blakeley was overrun. Men who had once been enslaved charged the earthworks of their former world and helped end it."
— The Conservation Fund / Historic Blakeley State Park
